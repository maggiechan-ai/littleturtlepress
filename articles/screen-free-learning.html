Screen-Free Learning: Building Vocabulary Through Books and Figurines

📵 9 min read · Little Turtle Press Research
← Back to Research

Let's start with an uncomfortable truth: the average American child under the age of 2 is now exposed to over an hour of screen time per day. By age 3, that number climbs to over two hours. These figures have been steadily rising for a decade, accelerated by the pandemic and the proliferation of tablets, smartphones, and "educational" apps marketed to ever-younger audiences.

Meanwhile, the research on screen time for very young children keeps pointing in the same direction: for children under 3, screens are a poor substitute for the real-world interactions that drive healthy development. Physical books, tangible toys, and face-to-face engagement with caregivers remain the gold standard for early learning.

This isn't a moral judgment about parents who use screens. It's a statement about neuroscience, developmental psychology, and what young brains actually need. At Little Turtle Press, we built our books to be the kind of screen-free, multi-sensory experience that the research says children need most.

What the AAP Actually Says

The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) — the most authoritative voice on children's health in the United States — has issued clear guidelines on screen time for young children:

  • Under 18 months: Avoid screen media other than video chatting with family members.
  • 18 to 24 months: If parents choose to introduce digital media, they should select high-quality programming and watch it with their children to help them understand what they're seeing.
  • 2 to 5 years: Limit screen use to one hour per day of high-quality programs. Parents should co-view with children.
Source: American Academy of Pediatrics. (2016). Media and Young Minds. Pediatrics, 138(5), e20162591. Updated guidance reaffirmed in subsequent AAP publications through 2024.

The reasoning behind these guidelines is rooted in decades of developmental research. The AAP's concern isn't that screens are inherently evil — it's that screen time displaces the kinds of interactions that are known to drive healthy development: face-to-face communication, physical play, book reading, and hands-on exploration of the physical world.

The "Video Deficit" Effect

One of the most consistent findings in developmental psychology is the video deficit effect: children under approximately 30 months learn less from video (including television, tablets, and apps) than from equivalent live interactions.

In a typical study, researchers teach toddlers a new skill or vocabulary word either through a live demonstration or through a video recording of the exact same demonstration. The live group consistently outperforms the video group — often by substantial margins.

"Children under 30 months consistently learn less from screens than from equivalent real-world interactions — a phenomenon researchers call the 'video deficit effect.' The younger the child, the larger the gap."

Why does this happen? Several factors contribute:

Lack of Contingent Interaction

When a parent reads a book, they respond to the child in real time. They pause when the child points. They follow the child's gaze. They adjust their language based on the child's responses. This contingent interaction — where the adult's behavior is responsive to the child's — is a powerful driver of learning that screens cannot replicate.

Even "interactive" apps fall short here. A touchscreen can respond to a tap, but it can't notice that the child is confused, or excited, or looking at something unexpected. It can't adjust its pace, repeat a word with different emphasis, or connect book content to the child's personal experience. A parent can do all of these things — and they do, naturally, during shared book reading.

Perceptual Poverty

A screen provides visual and auditory information — but only those two senses. There's no texture, no weight, no temperature, no smell. For young children who are still building their understanding of the physical world, this sensory limitation represents a significant loss of learning opportunity.

A physical book, by contrast, engages more senses than many parents realize. The child feels the weight of the book, the texture of the pages, the action of turning a page. These tactile experiences contribute to the overall learning experience in ways that a flat, smooth screen surface cannot.

And when you add a figurine to the mix? The sensory richness multiplies dramatically. Now the child has a three-dimensional object with weight, texture, and form — an object they can manipulate, explore, and connect to the visual content of the book.

The Displacement Problem

Perhaps the most significant concern about early screen time is not what screens do, but what they prevent. Every minute a toddler spends watching a screen is a minute they're not spending:

  • Being read to by a caregiver
  • Exploring objects with their hands
  • Having a face-to-face conversation
  • Engaging in imaginative play
  • Moving their bodies through physical space

These displaced activities are precisely the ones that research identifies as most important for early development. The issue isn't that 20 minutes of a well-made educational show is harmful — it's that those 20 minutes could have been spent in ways that produce stronger developmental outcomes.

Books vs. Screens: What the Comparison Studies Show

Several studies have directly compared learning outcomes from physical books versus digital equivalents, and the findings consistently favor physical books for very young children:

Key Study: Munzer, T. G., Miller, A. L., Weeks, H. M., Kaciroti, N., & Radesky, J. (2019). Differences in parent-toddler interactions with electronic versus print books. Pediatrics, 143(4), e20182012. This study found that parent-toddler pairs reading physical books engaged in more collaborative interaction, richer language, and more content-related discussion than those using electronic books on tablets.

The Munzer study is particularly revealing because it examined not just what children learned, but how parent-child pairs behaved during reading. With physical books:

  • Parents used more elaborative language — longer sentences, more complex vocabulary, more questions
  • There was more joint attention — parent and child focused on the same content simultaneously
  • Parents engaged in more content-related talk about the story or information
  • Children showed more engaged behavior and less distraction

With tablet-based e-books, by contrast:

  • Parents spent more time managing the device itself — "don't press that," "swipe this way," "wait, let me fix it"
  • Conversations shifted from the book's content to the technology
  • Children were more likely to be distracted by the device's other features
  • The overall reading experience was less collaborative and more fragmented

The physical book, it turns out, creates the conditions for better learning not just through its content but through the interaction patterns it encourages between parent and child.

Why Tactile Play Matters for Cognitive Development

The sense of touch is often called the "forgotten sense" in education, overshadowed by the emphasis on visual and auditory learning. But for young children, touch is arguably the most important learning channel of all.

From birth, children explore their world primarily through touch. They put things in their mouths. They grasp, squeeze, drop, and retrieve objects. They explore textures with their fingers. This isn't random behavior — it's systematic exploration that builds the child's understanding of physical properties like weight, texture, shape, size, and temperature.

Research in haptic perception (the science of touch) shows that tactile exploration of objects activates brain regions that are also involved in:

  • Spatial reasoning: Understanding three-dimensional relationships
  • Memory formation: Tactile memories are among the most durable
  • Language development: Words for physical properties (soft, hard, rough, smooth) are grounded in tactile experience
  • Problem-solving: Physical manipulation of objects builds causal reasoning

When a child holds a lion figurine while looking at a photograph of a real lion, they're building a concept of "lion" that includes visual information (what it looks like), auditory information (what it's called, what sounds it makes), AND tactile information (what it feels like to hold, its approximate shape and size). This multi-dimensional concept is richer, more stable, and more accessible than one built on visual and auditory information alone.

"Touch is not a lesser sense — it's a foundational one. For young children, hands-on exploration builds the cognitive architecture that all later learning rests upon."

The "Educational" App Myth

Walk through the App Store's children's section and you'll find thousands of apps claiming to be "educational." Many feature animal content strikingly similar to what you'd find in a good picture book: photographs, vocabulary words, sounds, and facts.

So why not just use an app instead of a book?

The research suggests several reasons:

1. Hot Spots and Distraction

Interactive apps are designed with "hot spots" — areas children can tap for animations, sounds, or games. Studies show that these interactive features, while entertaining, actually distract from the educational content rather than enhancing it. Children spend more time tapping hot spots than engaging with the actual learning material.

2. Passive Consumption Patterns

Despite being called "interactive," most children's apps encourage a pattern of passive consumption — tap, watch, tap, watch — that's fundamentally different from the active, self-directed exploration that occurs with physical objects. A figurine can be held in unlimited ways; an app responds only to pre-programmed inputs.

3. No Caregiver Integration

Apps are typically designed for solo use by the child. Physical books are inherently social — they require (or at least strongly encourage) a caregiver's participation. This shared experience is where the richest learning happens, through the conversational exchanges that dialogic reading promotes.

4. Reduced Transfer

As with cartoon images (discussed in our article on real photographs vs. cartoons), learning from screens often fails to transfer to real-world contexts. A child who learns to identify a lion on a screen may not recognize the same lion in a book, at the zoo, or as a figurine. Real-world learning tools produce real-world learning.

A Note on Parent Guilt

If your child watches some screens, you are not failing as a parent. Let's be absolutely clear about that. Screens are a reality of modern life, and there are legitimate reasons why parents use them — from cooking dinner in peace to managing a cross-country flight.

The goal isn't to eliminate all screens. It's to ensure that the majority of a young child's learning experiences are grounded in the real-world interactions that research shows are most effective. Every minute you spend reading a book with your child, playing with figurines together, or having a conversation about what you see — that's a minute invested in your child's development in the most effective way science has identified.

Our books are designed to make those minutes as rich, as easy, and as enjoyable as possible — for both parent and child.

The Bottom Line

Screens are everywhere. But for the critical 0–3 developmental window, the evidence is clear: physical books, tangible toys, and real human interaction produce the strongest learning outcomes. Not because screens are bad, but because real-world, multi-sensory experiences are that much better.

A Read & Play book paired with an animal figurine gives your child something no screen can: the chance to see it, hear about it, and hold it in their hands — all at once, all in the real world, all shared with someone who loves them. That's not just learning. That's the best kind of learning there is. 🐢
