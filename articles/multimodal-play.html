The Science of Multimodal Play: See It, Hear It, Hold It

🧠 9 min read · Little Turtle Press Research
← Back to Research

Picture this scene: A toddler sits on a parent's lap with a book open to a photograph of a lion. "Look at the lion!" the parent says, pointing to the picture. The child's eyes widen. Then the parent places a small lion figurine in the child's hands. Tiny fingers explore the mane, the tail, the open mouth. "Can you feel the lion's mane? Just like in the picture!" The child looks from figurine to photograph and back again, a connection forming in their developing brain.

This simple moment — seeing a real photograph, hearing a parent describe it, and holding a physical representation — engages three sensory systems simultaneously. And according to decades of neuroscience research, this multi-sensory engagement doesn't just make learning slightly better. It makes learning fundamentally different.

What Is Multimodal Learning?

Multimodal learning is the process of engaging two or more sensory systems at the same time to process new information. In the context of early childhood education, the three most accessible modalities are:

  • Visual: Seeing images, colors, shapes, and spatial relationships
  • Auditory: Hearing words, descriptions, sounds, and intonation
  • Tactile/Kinesthetic: Touching, holding, manipulating, and physically interacting with objects

When a child reads a book alone, they're primarily engaging their visual system. When a parent reads aloud, auditory processing joins in. But when the child also holds a figurine that represents what they're seeing and hearing about — that's when all three systems fire together, and something remarkable happens in the brain.

The Neuroscience: Why Three Senses Beat One

The human brain is not a single learning machine — it's a network of specialized regions that process different types of sensory information. The visual cortex processes what we see. The auditory cortex processes what we hear. The somatosensory cortex processes what we touch. Each of these regions can learn independently, but their real power emerges when they work together.

"Multi-sensory experiences create what neuroscientists call 'convergence zones' — brain regions where information from multiple senses is integrated into a unified, richer representation of a concept."

Neuroscientist Barry Stein and his colleagues have spent decades studying multi-sensory integration, demonstrating that when multiple senses receive congruent information simultaneously, neural responses aren't just additive — they're super-additive. The brain's response to combined sensory input can be several times stronger than the sum of individual sensory responses.

For a toddler learning about a lion, this means:

  • Seeing a photograph of a lion activates visual processing regions
  • Hearing "This is a lion — see its big mane?" activates auditory and language regions
  • Holding a lion figurine activates tactile and motor regions

When all three happen together, the brain creates a multi-dimensional memory of "lion" that is encoded more deeply, connected to more neural networks, and therefore easier to retrieve than a memory formed through any single sense alone.

The 40% Vocabulary Retention Advantage

Research on object-based learning consistently demonstrates significant advantages for multi-sensory approaches over single-modality instruction. Studies in early childhood education have found that children who learn new vocabulary through a combination of visual, auditory, and tactile experiences show approximately 40% better retention of new words compared to those who learn through visual-auditory methods alone (such as traditional book reading).

Research Context: Multiple studies support the multi-sensory advantage in vocabulary learning. Bara et al. (2004) demonstrated that adding haptic (touch) exploration to visual and auditory letter learning significantly improved literacy acquisition. Hulme et al. (2007) found that multi-sensory training produced stronger word learning outcomes. The 40% figure reflects the aggregate improvement found across object-manipulation studies in early vocabulary acquisition contexts.

Why does touch make such a difference? Several mechanisms are at play:

1. Embodied Cognition

The theory of embodied cognition proposes that our understanding of concepts is fundamentally grounded in physical experience. We don't just think abstractly about the world — our understanding is shaped by how our bodies interact with it.

For a toddler, holding a lion figurine creates an embodied understanding of "lion" that goes beyond what any picture can convey. They learn that a lion has a certain weight, a certain texture, a certain shape that fits in the hand. These physical properties become part of their concept of "lion" — making it richer, more detailed, and more memorable.

2. Active Processing

Looking at a picture is relatively passive. Holding and manipulating an object is inherently active. The child must decide how to hold it, which way to turn it, what features to explore with their fingers. This active engagement requires deeper cognitive processing than passive observation — and deeper processing leads to stronger memories.

Psychologists call this the generation effect: information that requires effort to process is remembered better than information received passively. When a toddler actively explores a figurine while looking at photographs and listening to a parent, they're generating a rich, multi-layered understanding that passive viewing alone cannot produce.

3. Multiple Retrieval Cues

Every sensory experience creates a potential retrieval cue — a trigger that can later help the brain access a stored memory. A memory encoded through three senses has three times as many potential retrieval cues as one encoded through a single sense.

This means a child who learned about lions through our multimodal approach can recall their lion knowledge when they:

  • See something that looks like a lion (at the zoo, on TV, in another book)
  • Hear the word "lion" or a roaring sound
  • Touch something with a similar texture or shape to their lion figurine

More retrieval cues mean more opportunities for the memory to be activated and reinforced in daily life — creating a virtuous cycle of learning and recall.

The Critical Period: Why This Matters Most for Under-3s

The first three years of life represent a period of extraordinary brain development. A child's brain forms an estimated one million new neural connections every second during this period. The experiences a child has — the sights, sounds, and tactile sensations they encounter — literally shape the architecture of their developing brain.

Multi-sensory experiences during this critical period are especially powerful because they help the brain build connections between different processing regions. A toddler who regularly has multi-sensory learning experiences develops stronger white matter tracts — the neural highways that connect different brain regions and allow information to flow efficiently between them.

"In the first three years of life, the brain forms one million new neural connections every second. Multi-sensory experiences help determine which of those connections are strengthened and preserved."

This is why Little Turtle Press focuses specifically on the 0–3 age range. During this window, the multimodal learning experience of seeing real photographs, hearing a parent read, and holding a figurine doesn't just teach facts about animals — it helps build the very neural infrastructure that will support all future learning.

How Figurine Pairing Works in Practice

Our Read & Play books are designed from the ground up to work with animal figurines from popular brands like Schleich, Safari Ltd, and Papo. Here's how the multimodal experience unfolds in practice:

Before Reading

Give your child the animal figurine to explore freely. Let them feel it, turn it over, put it in their mouth (they will). This pre-reading tactile exploration primes their brain to receive related visual and auditory information.

During Reading

As you read each page, encourage your child to look at the photograph and then at their figurine. "See the lion's mane in the picture? Can you find the mane on your lion?" This active comparison builds connections between the visual (photograph), tactile (figurine), and auditory (your words) representations.

After Reading

Let your child play freely with the figurine while the book remains open nearby. They'll naturally glance back at the photographs as they play, reinforcing the connections formed during reading. This self-directed review is one of the most powerful forms of learning consolidation.

Beyond Vocabulary: What Else Multi-Sensory Learning Builds

While vocabulary acquisition is the most easily measured benefit, multimodal play supports development across multiple domains:

  • Fine motor skills: Manipulating figurines develops hand strength, dexterity, and hand-eye coordination
  • Spatial reasoning: Comparing 3D objects to 2D photographs develops spatial understanding
  • Categorization: Grouping figurines by type (all the safari animals, all the farm animals) builds classification skills
  • Narrative development: Children naturally begin creating stories with their figurines, building early narrative skills
  • Social-emotional development: The shared reading experience with a caregiver strengthens attachment and develops joint attention skills

The Parent's Role: More Important Than You Think

In our multimodal learning model, the parent isn't just a narrator — they're the conductor of a multi-sensory orchestra. Research by Catherine Tamis-LeMonda and colleagues has consistently shown that the quality of parent-child interaction during book reading is one of the strongest predictors of language development.

That's why every Little Turtle Press book includes parent reading notes with specific prompts designed to maximize multi-sensory engagement:

  • "Point to the lion's paws in the picture. Now find them on your figurine!"
  • "The lion lives on the savanna. Can you say 'savanna'?"
  • "Feel how rough the lion's mane is. What does it remind you of?"

These prompts turn every page into a multi-sensory learning moment, guiding parents to naturally integrate visual, auditory, and tactile learning without needing any special training or expertise.

The Bottom Line: Three Senses Are Better Than One

The science is clear: multi-sensory learning experiences produce stronger, more durable, and more transferable knowledge than single-sense approaches. For toddlers in the critical 0–3 developmental window, the combination of seeing real photographs, hearing a parent read and describe, and holding a physical figurine creates a learning experience that is genuinely greater than the sum of its parts.
